Why you should listen

"Belle and Sebastian...aren't they that band that's mentioned in 500 Days of Summer?". Yes, yes they are.

Aside from that, they're also a delighful band from Scotland who have made some of the best and most quaint indie music of all time. Many of their albums have seen critical success but this album is often regarded as their masterpiece, and it's not hard to see why.

Every song on sinister tells a picturesque story, whether that be one of love (Seeing Other People), of yearning (Judy and the Dream of Horses), or even of generational differences (Me and the Major). These stories are complimented by beautiful instrumentation from all 8 members of the band who were on the album. Although there were quite a few band members, it never feels as if anyone is fighting for attention; every drum beat, guitar twang, and trumpet appear where they're needed and don't outstay their welcome.

A lot of the compliments this album receives can be attributed to other Belle and Sebastian albums, however what makes this album stand out is that it's all killer, no filler. Not a single second is wasted across these 10 tracks.

Favourite tracks: Get Me Away From Here, I'm Dying, Me and the Major, Judy and the Dream of Horses

Why you should listen

The phrase 'instant classic' gets thrown around a lot by music fans and especially critics, but rarely has an album been given such praise and also deserved it as this absolute masterpiece.

This album has been said to be the last great rock album, and while I don't agree with that, it's easy to see how important this album was, and still is, to rock music. The production alone on this album would be enough to rant on for three pages about, what with it being so rich and containing so many little details that reward subsequent re-listens. From the sinister strings on Climbing Up the Walls to the glockenspiel on No Surprises, this album beyond transends the norms of what you might associate with a traditional rock album, especially onw from the 90's.

People have tried to argue that this album is a concept album about technology, what with the title and the robotic voice on Fitter Happier, and some lines here and there, however even the band disagrees with this assessment. Something that I see links all the lyrics is a commantary on the human psyche. There are songs about how you feel after surviving an accident (Airbag, Lucky),mental anguish (Climbing Up the Walls, No Surprises), and even politics (Electioneering). They may be abstract, but Thom Yorke's lyrics pack a mean punch and help sell the futuristic hellscape this album is alluding to.

There will never be another album quite as impactful and excellent as OK Computer, and you know what? That's fine. We already have an OK Computer, and it's amazing.

Favourite Tracks: No Surpises, Let Down, Climbing Up the Walls

Once upon a time, MF DOOM decided to make an album about food, and it was incredible.

MF DOOM, your favourite rapper's favourite rapper. Imagine you're DOOM in 2004: You've established yourself as a legendary underground rapper with your debut album Operation Doomsday, and then only a few months prior to now you had released Madvillany, arguably one of the best rap albums of all time with one of the best producers of all time? Apparently you just decide your hungry and write songs about food.

Don't get it twisted though, MM..FOOD isn't just DOOM spitting bars about his favourite snacks, he uses food as a medium to tell multiple stories about loss (Kon Karne), fake friends (Deep Fried Friendz), and even cyer sex (Kookies). The endless food puns and metaphors would be a challenge to most rappers, but DOOM gives the impression that this was easy for him to do. Also just the thought of him using a song called "Beef Rap" to describe rap beef is hilarious in and of itself.

As well as being lyrically impressive, DOOM's production on this album is stellar; it almost seems like he saw how impressive Madlib was on Madvillainy and wished to top it. He's a master of using eclectic samples from a range of places, both musical and not (it's a real shame he had to remove the Sesame Street sample from Kookies), to create memorable instrumentals that complement his non-stop rhymes. This is DOOM at his villanous best.

Why you should listen

Absolution on paper sounds a depressing album. The first song is "Apocalypse Please", there are several songs regarding unrequited love, and even some more harrowing topics such a stockholm syndrome and trying coming to terms with your death. But not only is the album not depressing, it's also an alt rock masterpiece.

After the critical and comercial success of 2001's Origin of Symmetry, it would have been an easy assumption that Muse wouldn't have been able to follow it up; they probably would just play it safe or something. No. Absolution incorpates new sounds that the previous two albums didn't have (one track is even a full-on metal track), and increases the tacklist number from Origin, without losing ay of the consistency.

This album's flow is incredible; you go from the madness of Hysteria, to the depressive contemplating of Blackout, to the classical sound of Butterflies and Hurricanes, and it's never jarring. How Matt Bellamy and co crafted so many excellent tracks, and then sequenced them in such a way that listening to them is a breeze is an incredible feat.

Each band member is on top form on here. Chris and Dom's bass playing and drumming respectivley are usually in the background to Bellamy's guitar/piano, but they really shine here. The best instance of this is Hysteria: if you took a single element of that track out then it would be nowhere near as good and would go from one of the best tracks to maybe the worst.

This is Muse's best album, and one of the bets rock albums of the 2000's

"But to look past the crudness of children is to attain tru knowledge and wisdom". That's what it says on the booklet inside The Moldy Peaches CD case. It summarises the album quite well; on the surface this album might seem incredibly childish and crude, but if you listen closely and with an open mind, you'll see that this album has many layers, and that Kimya Dawson and Adam Green are far more clever than they're presenting themselves are.

As crude as it may be, this album deals with many things including unrequited love (Nothing Came Out), an alien invasion (What Went Wrong), and burgers that may or may not contain drugs (These Brugers). Steak for Chicken is probably the funniest song on the entire album, what with its joyful attitude and rediculous double verses. Nothing Came Out is heartbreakingly sweet, what with it's story of a girl just wanting to watch Scooby Doo and hold hands with her crush.

All of the inrtuments are purposefully low-budget, and this fits well with the lyrics as you can just imagine Dawson and Green as teenagers playing in their parents' garage, recording the album with their phone. Don't be trciked into thinking there's isn't musical talent here however: there are plenty of interesting guitar pieces, and the synths on The Ballad of Hellen Keller & Rip Van Winkle sound heavenly.

The charcters in The Moldy Peaches might not have grown up, but there's more to life than that, and this album is proof.

Why you should listen

Music isn't always entertainment. That might sound like an odd thing to say, especially given the context of this site being to recommend albums for you to listen to, but I'd be painting a false picture if I said that this album is made for entertainment.

Mount Eerie is one of many projects from aclaimed singer/songwriter/multi-instrumentalist Phil Elverum. This album shares many similarities with his other albums: sparse instrumentals, nature references, and incredible melodies. What's different however is the subject matter. Phil's wife of 12 years, Geneviève Castrée, lost her year-long battle with cancer. I don't think I need to say that this was devestating for Phil, but what's unique is the way he chose to express his morning, mainly by creating this album.

There have been many songs and albums about death and loss, but what makes this album different is the fact that Phil takes a great effort not to romanticise death, at all. There are several lyrics alluding to this, such as "It's dumb, I don't want to learn anything from this", and as you can imagine, many lyrics about his grieving and how its affecting him. I won't spoil any more as I don't want to take away what this album has to offer.

Some people have argued that this isn't even music, and I can see why people would think that; the music clearly does take a backseat to the lyrics. However I think the instrumentals are still excellently produced, and music is all about expressing yourself, and this is one of the most unique expressions of the 2010's. This album is almost like a collection of diary entries with beautiful folk music supplimenting them.

Like I said, this album might not entertain you, but I'd recommend it if only for it being such a unique expression of grief. Hope you're doing better, Phil.

Favourite tracks: Real Death, Toothbrush/Trash, Crow

Pink Floyd

Wish You Were Here

Tracklist

  1. Shine On You Crazy Diamond (Parts 1-5)
  2. Welcome to the Machine
  3. Have a Cigar
  4. Wish You Were Here
  5. Shine On You Crazy Diamond (Parts 6-9)

Why you should listen

How do you follow up one of, if not the, best album of all time?

That was a question critics and fans were asking after Pink Floyd's 8th studio album The Dark Side of the Moon, an album so critically and comercially successful that it set the bar for rock music as a whole, never mind for just Pink Floyd. This expectation initially led to a huge dissapointment with this album: to go from Dark Side something that seemed much less grand was not what fans expected nor wanted.

Thankfully, over the years Wish You Were Here has been recognised for the masterpiece it is. WYWH is the amalgamation of eveything great about Floyd: you have the long prog rock instrumentals the band is famous for, along with brilliant concepts, and over-the-top style. This is a perfect starter album if you haven't heard any Pink Floyd before, and if you become a Floyd fan this album becomes even better once you learn all the backstory to this album.

The lyrics on Have a Cigar and Welcome to the Machine are thinly-veiled attacks on the music industry as a whole, and provide a false sense of security before the emotional gut-punch of the title track, which deserves to be one of Floyds most popular songs because of its sparse but excellent instrumental and the relatable lyrics.

Similar to the business man on the front cover, Pink floyd were on fire with this album.

Favourite tracks: Welcome to the Machine, Have a Cigar, Wish You Were Here

Scenery

  1. It Could Happen To You
  2. I Want To Talk About You
  3. Early Summer
  4. Willow Weep For Me

Why you should listen

There's a feeling thta I'm sure almost every person on the planet has experienced at some point, and that's that "I'm too old to start this": whether that be a new skill or trade, at some point we all compare ourselves to the people who started when they were 5 years old. If Ryo Fukui had had the same mindset when he started learning the piano at the age of 22, we wouldn't have been blessed with some of the best jazz albums of the past 50 years.

Jazz had its hayday back in the west in the 50's and 60's, and as such by the time Japan caught up, the rest of the world had moved on. It's because of that that such albums like this have largely gone unnoticed amongst the "true" jazz fans. That's a huge shame as they're missing out on 6 beautiful tracks.

Fukui's lack of formal education shows, but I mean that as a compliment: there's certain "rules" you learn as a musician, and as such it can be hard to even think to break them, but Scenery sounds like Fukui is just playing what comes naturally to him and as such the songs sounds a lot more free form than even most songs that are going for that. Just because he's a pianist, Fukui isn't afraid to let the other musicians shine: the drum solo on the first half of the album is a real highlight; it isn't flashy but it's exciting enough to keep your attention.

Ryo Fukui may not be remembered along with the jazz greats such as Miles Davis, John Coltrane, or Herbie Hancock, but hopefully his music will live on.

Favourite tracks: It Could Happen to You, Willow Weep For Me, Scenery

When you think of "the best current rappers", or the best rappers in general, it's unlikely that any from the UK come to mind, even if you're from the UK yourself. Hopefully that'll change once you listen to this 2019 album from Little Simz.

Simz had been releasing albums for years, gaining quite a bit of attention with her second album Stillness in Wonderland, but it almost seems like with this album that her previous efforts were mere warmp-ups: starting from this album she decided that every track will get amazing beats and production, instrospective lyrics, and effortless flows.

This album is basically 100% fundamentals to the point where it's hard to find faults but also simultaneously hard to come up with any praise that isn't just "it's an excellent album", which it certainly is. This album has an amazing flow; one minute your listening the anthemic background vocals in Boss, and before you know it you're immersed in the jazzy, soul-crushing sax of Flowers.

Simz is on top of her game here, and she knows it. The first half has a very "Fuck you, I'm the best" type of attitude with the songs Offence, Boss, and Venom. The latter song especially has angry lyrics that are...well, venomous. The second half of the album overall is a lot more introspective; she isn't afraid to talk about more personal topics such as her failed relationship (Sherbert Sunset), her mental health (Therapy), and the fear that she'll die young like the other greats (Flowers). This contrast goes to show how far Simz has come as a lyrical storyteller.

Little Simz is the best response to "Does the UK actually have any good hip hop?"

Art Blakey

Moanin

Tracklist

  1. Moanin
  2. Are You Real
  3. Along Came Betty
  4. The Drum Thunder Suite
  5. Blues March
  6. Come Rain or Come Shine

Art Blakey has a weird place when it comes to classic jazz artists. He's wildly considered to be a fantastic drummer and band leader, however he isn't talked about along with greats such as Davis or Coltrane. I think in this is in part due to him being a drummer, an instrument that isn't as associated as much with jazz compared to a trumpet or saxophone, and also because his albums focused on just being excellent as opposed to being ground-breaking.

The album's title track open up the album and that intro is iconic, it's such a memorable trumpet line and teh way the song builds from it and then circles back to it is astounding. That and Are You Real have been credited as getting a lot of people, myself included, into the jazz genre itself, and it's easy to see why; the music gets stuck in your head and has such an incredible pacing throughout that you don't need to be able to be a jazz fan to appreciate and enjoy.

As much of an amaing drummer as he was, Blakey knew when to sit back and just play the rythm and let the othe members shine, which can be quite a hard thing to do when you're that talented; your ego is bound to take over sometimes. But when he does shine, he shines, his solos range from technical to simple, while keeping constant power.

Favourite songs: Moanin, Are You Real, The Drum Thunder Suite
